01

The Mark

The flame-trident, set in a bronze medallion. It stands for the Order the way a name stands for a person: not decorative, but load-bearing.

Construction

The mark is built from three flames rising from a single base: one tall and central, two shorter and outward-leaning. Read together they form a trident, an antler, a rising fire. All three readings are intentional. Behind the flames sits a four-point star, the compass mark of the Order's territorial claim: Northeast, ascendant.

Minimum size

Never render the medallion smaller than 28px / 0.4in. Below this the flame tines lose separation and the mark reads as a smear, not a symbol.

Clearspace

Maintain clearspace equal to the height of one flame tine (roughly 20% of the medallion diameter) on all sides. Nothing else, no wordmark, no rule line, no photograph, crosses this boundary.

02

The Archetypes

Three masculine forms the Order stands upon: the Father, the Striker, and the Lord of the Earth. Each carries its own glyph, cut in black for parchment and reversed in white for bronze and stone.

Father
Solar Vision

The wheel and the open eye. Sight as authority: what the Father looks upon, he governs. Radial spokes read as both sunburst and iris, watchfulness given the shape of the sun.

Striker
Double Vajra

Crossed thunderbolts at the center of a compass rose. Force applied at a single decisive point, the strike that clears ground for what comes after.

Lord of the Earth
Tree of Life

Branch and root along a single trunk, six-fold and symmetric. Growth that answers to gravity, the archetype that keeps the other two from floating free of the ground.

Usage

Archetype glyphs identify a role, a rite, or a member's standing, never a decoration filling empty space. Use the black mark on Ivory or Parchment, the reversed mark on Earth or Void.

03

Canonized Glyphs

Nine marks canonized by the Order, each a compressed statement of doctrine, cut into seals, records, and gathering objects wherever words are too many.

Animus Accensus
Meaning

Inspired by the concept of animus as the masculine Roman soul, it translates to a masculine soul that has been set on fire with inspiration to perform virtuous deeds and attain honor.

Thrakos
Meaning

Represents the "Principle of Action." It signifies that the male role is fundamentally active rather than passive — a man must perform actions to define his identity and presence.

Ianoos
Meaning

Pronounced Ya-NOOS. Named after Janus, the Roman god of gates, passages, beginnings, and endings, traditionally portrayed with two faces. It symbolizes doorways, thresholds, and perimeters.

Soma
Meaning

Symbolizes the offering or sacrifice, closely associated with a ritual libation. It serves as the answer to the question, "What would you sacrifice?", representing a melding of the familiar and the new to forge culture.

Toros
Meaning

Represents the Bull (or Bvll), which is a core symbolic animal in solar culture and the culture of the Order. It grounds the more abstract glyphs in a powerful, physical symbol of strength.

Mitra
Meaning

Represents the balance between Earth and Sky. Symbolically, it stands for the role of man in balancing high ideals with practical reality, acknowledging that man is an animal rooted in the physical world.

Artas
Meaning

Used to symbolize future events and the shared experiences within them. It represents how distinct individuals are brought together by their alignment on fundamental ideals, values, and goals.

Gwitas
Meaning

Grounded in the philosophy that "Life is Differentiation." It serves as a symbolic method for processing, navigating, and celebrating the concepts of life and death.

Iove Fulgente
Meaning

Translates to "The Thunderbolt Rules All Things." It emphasizes action and self-reliance over passivity, rejecting the concept of begging or mere asking in favor of taking decisive, meaningful action.

The 90 / 8 / 2 rule

A page built correctly reads as roughly 90% Stone and Metal, 8% Fire & Sky accents, and 2% pure ignition color (Greek Orange or Weathered Tyrian) at the single moment that deserves it. If a page feels loud, the ratio has been broken, not the palette.

Solar Aesthetics

A working theory, not a decree: what "solar" means visually, where it comes from, and the artists whose work develops it.

Some have asked for a unifying theory of solar aesthetics, and I have always been hesitant to create one, because I don't think my personal tastes and influences are necessarily the only tastes and influences that could broadly be considered "solar."

Really, anything masculine, powerful, aesthetically cohesive or well-ordered, and not overly focused on death, decay, or the mundane could be "solar." We often reference the classics, so art from the classical world feels very solar, but so does Egyptian and Mesopotamian art.

That said, even angry or ugly art could have its place, especially in the context of the Striker and his relationship to the Dragon or "monsters."

However, as a movement, I do think there are some concepts that have influenced me aesthetically which are also conceptually aligned with our project.

If anything, Solar Idealism is about mining the past for common themes, pulling the best or most ideal elements together, and adapting them for the world and the time we live in. For instance, there is the throughline of the campfire and the stone circle that connects what we do with what men have done for thousands of years, all the way back into prehistory.

Bones, stones, cave drawings, archetypal animals, mountains, the sun, a connection to the human form and body are all reference points that transcend individual cultures, eras, and locales.

One artist who was an early influence on me was Isamu Noguchi, a Japanese American sculptor, furniture designer, and theater set designer with an aesthetic that is sometimes referred to as archaic modernism.

Archaic Modernism

Archaic modernism describes an aesthetic that fuses the primal, timeless qualities of ancient or prehistoric art with the streamlined, minimalist principles of mid-20th-century modernism. It evokes the raw monumentality of megaliths, primitive totems, ancient stone carvings, or mythic forms—while abstracting them into clean, geometric, or organic shapes suited to modern materials, industrial processes, and public scale.

This approach, prominent in the late 1950s and early 1960s, rejects ornate decoration in favor of essential, elemental expression. Artists like Isamu Noguchi exemplified it by drawing from Egyptian pyramids, Zen gardens, Buddhist temples, and American Indian mounds, then distilling those archaic inspirations into smooth, abstract sculptures, furniture, and landscapes that feel both ancient and forward-looking. The result creates a "stylistic wormhole" linking deep past to distant future—timeless yet innovative, grounded in raw materiality (stone, concrete, metal) yet aspiring to relativity and universality in an Einsteinian sense.

In broader terms, it aligns with brutalist tendencies: unadorned surfaces, massive forms, and a sense of enduring gravity. For Solar Idealism, archaic modernism offers a visual language that is future-primitive—remixing primal mythic past into a bold, ordered vision of masculine vitality, light over chaos, and eternal becoming. It rejects postmodern fragmentation for a dynamic gravitas: bright, noble, virile, and uplifting.
